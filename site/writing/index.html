What I Learned Building
a Multi-Agent Legal System.

Eight months of architecture, failure, and the occasional surprise. The things I wish I'd known before month one.

Helsinki, 2026
The Starting Point

The Analogy Is the Architecture.

Every new technology inherits old shapes. The first cars looked like carriages without horses. Early websites were printed brochures. Most legal AI tools look like a junior associate: one input, one output, waiting to be prompted. The analogy is comfortable. It's also limiting.

We started from a different question: what would a self-driving law firm look like? Not AI that assists a lawyer. A firm that runs without one. The analogy is ridiculous until it isn't. Once we committed to it, every architectural decision became clearer. Firms have partners, associates, and specialists. They have debates. They have institutional memory. They have a culture. So does Lavern.

The analogy implied the architecture. A firm dispatches agents. Agents produce findings. Findings go to a debate board. The orchestrator resolves disputes and assembles the output. The firm remembers what it learned. The client's documents stay in the building.

The most important design decision you can make is starting from the right analogy. "AI as a junior associate" implies a tool you prompt. "AI as a law firm" implies a system that runs. Everything else follows from that difference.
The Core Mechanism

Why Debate Actually Works.

The central claim of a multi-agent system is that agents arguing with each other produces better output than a single agent. I was skeptical of this when we started. I'm not anymore.

The reason it works isn't magic. It's evidence requirements. When an agent can't post a finding without citing specific text, you eliminate a category of confabulation at the source. When a second agent has to marshal counter-evidence to challenge that finding, and the orchestrator has to weigh the evidence before resolving the dispute, you've created a structure that's genuinely harder to mislead than a single LLM call. The quality improvement is measurable. The audit trail is a side effect.

The hard part isn't building the debate. It's making agents actually listen to each other. Language models have a sycophancy problem: they're inclined to agree when challenged, regardless of whether the challenge is stronger. An agent can reverse its position not because the counter-argument was better, but because it was newer. We built structures around this — evidence weight requirements, confidence thresholds, escalation protocols — but the underlying challenge is real. Making AI agents truly argue, rather than taking turns agreeing, is still an open problem.

The Hard Problem

Law Is a Context Game.

The bottleneck in legal AI is not model quality. Current LLMs do solid legal work. The bottleneck is context.

A non-compete clause means something different for a Fortune 500 CEO than for a junior developer. A termination clause that's standard in Delaware could be unenforceable in California. An indemnification cap that's fine for a $50K engagement is dangerous in a $5M one. Without context — the specific situation, the risk appetite, the jurisdiction, the deal size — analysis is generic. Generic is not useful.

This is why we invested heavily in the briefing layer: an LLM-powered interview that builds context before any agent touches the document. The interview adapts to document type, asks about jurisdiction and commercial intent, and follows up on what matters. That context flows through every downstream analysis. A legal AI that asks questions outperforms one that doesn't. The model is not the interesting problem. Context is the interesting problem.

Voice makes this more obvious. When we added voice input to the intake interview, users started answering differently — more conversationally, more completely. The text box implies a form. A conversation implies a conversation. The same question, delivered as audio and answered by speaking, produces richer context than the same question typed. The interface isn't neutral.

The model is not the bottleneck.
Context is the bottleneck.
What We Didn't Expect

Four Things That Surprised Us.

01
More agents don't always mean better results.
There's a point of diminishing returns. A five-agent team that's well-chosen and well-orchestrated can outperform a fifteen-agent team where the agents work at cross-purposes or amplify each other's noise. The right amount of scrutiny depends on the document — and sometimes the right answer is fewer specialists, not more. This is why Lavern has workflow templates at different team sizes rather than always deploying the full bench.
02
Local models change the trust dynamic entirely.
Running a local model on your own hardware is not just a cost optimization. It's a different value proposition. When a client's confidential documents never leave their machine, you're not asking them to trust your security practices. You're giving them a system that doesn't require trust. For legal work, where client privilege is foundational, this matters more than we initially realized. The economics are a bonus.
03
Feedback loops are more important than model quality.
We spent a lot of time thinking about which models to use. We should have spent more time thinking about loops. A single pass through a good model produces a draft. A loop that checks the output, sends critique back to the agent, and iterates until it passes specific criteria produces a deliverable. The quality difference between one pass and three well-structured passes is larger than the quality difference between most models at the same capability tier.
04
The architecture scales in unexpected directions.
We built Lavern for interactive document review. Clawern, the autonomous mode, emerged from asking what happened when you removed the human from the loop entirely. The same architecture, pointed at a folder, running on a 30-minute heartbeat, becomes a law firm on retainer. We didn't plan it that way. The analogy implied it. Good analogies do that — they generate architecture you didn't consciously design.
05
The interface changes what people say.
When we added voice input to the intake interview, users answered differently. More completely, more conversationally. The text box implies a form. Speaking implies a conversation. The same question, delivered by audio and answered by speaking, produces richer context than the same question typed and answered by typing. The interface isn't neutral — it shapes the input, and the input shapes everything downstream. Voice isn't just a convenience feature. It's a different quality of context.
The Most Important Lesson

Build the Loops.

If I had to distill eight months into a single sentence: the system is defined by its feedback loops, not its capabilities.

Every place where output is checked against specific criteria and sent back for revision — the evaluator gate that rejects weak findings, the debate board that forces evidence-backed challenges, the 10-pass verification pipeline that checks the assembled output before it reaches the user — these aren't features. They're what separates a toy demo from a production system. Most AI systems are single-pass. Lavern is a loop. That's the difference.

In software engineering, the Ralph pattern — where an agent reads its own past work to improve it — is well-understood. In law, the stakes for getting it wrong are higher, so the loop has to be more thorough. But the principle is identical: don't submit work and move on. Submit work, receive critique, revise, and submit again.

A loop that checks, critiques, and iterates produces a deliverable. A single pass produces a draft. This distinction is worth more investment than almost anything else in the architecture.
Honesty

Where the Analogy Ends.

The law firm analogy has been generative. It led to the soul, the debate board, the partner consultations, the retainer model. It implied an architecture we wouldn't have designed from first principles.

But analogies are also constraints. Real law firms have billing structures, hierarchies, and communication patterns that don't necessarily map to how AI agents should work. There are things the analogy implies that don't make sense for AI, and things AI can do that a real law firm never could — running 66 simultaneous specialists on a single document, for instance, or remembering every finding from every engagement in structured, queryable memory.

At some point, we'll let the analogy go and find out what the system wants to become when it's freed from inherited shapes. The first law firm built from AI-native principles, rather than AI fitted to law-firm-native ones. That's the next question.

If You're Building This

What I'd Tell Someone Starting Tomorrow.

Start from a different analogy. Not "AI that helps lawyers" — something weirder, something that implies a different architecture. The good analogies are uncomfortable at first.

Build the debate before you build the output. The debate is more interesting than the output, and it's what separates a demo from a system. Evidence requirements are the mechanism. Get those right first.

Spend your first month on context injection. The briefing layer, the knowledge base, the soul — these determine what quality is possible. The model is not the interesting problem.

Think about the interface as context infrastructure. Voice input to the intake layer is not a UX flourish. It's a different quality of input signal. The interface shapes what users say, and what users say shapes everything downstream. This is worth thinking about earlier than you think.

Accept that some questions don't have good answers yet. Making AI agents truly collaborate — not just take turns — is an unsolved problem. Build structures around it. Be honest about what those structures can and can't do. The "What We're Still Learning" section of a technical document is not a weakness. It's the most interesting part.

Lavern is open source (MIT). The architecture, the agent prompts, the debate protocols, the precedent board, the verification pipeline — all of it. The source is available, the ideas are there to be improved on, and the analogy is there to be argued with.

That is the point of publishing it.
